Subthesis 3: Facing the Past Through Symbolism

The third subthesis evidence from Suzume comes from the scene at 00:45:00, where one of the main characters, Souta, explains the concept of Closers. He says:

“Closers have to shut doors that are left open. Those doors lead to the Ever After. If we don’t close them, disasters happen. The Worm slips out… and chaos spreads.”

While this idea of magical doors and worms may seem abstract, it carries strong symbolic meaning. The doors represent unresolved trauma and buried emotions. If left open, these emotional wounds can overflow into everyday life, causing pain and disruption—just like the worm in the film, which escapes and brings about natural disasters. The Ever After is not just a fantasy world, but a space that holds everything people try to forget or hide. When a door to that space opens, it becomes a moment where emotional pain resurfaces and demands to be addressed (The Conversation; DoctorKev).

Suzume’s new role as a Closer is important because it shows that healing from grief or trauma is not something that just happens with time. It takes effort, courage, and responsibility. She has to face painful memories, often alone, and make the choice to close the door each time. This is not just about stopping disasters in the world of the film. It mirrors the emotional work people must do to move on from loss. Her journey makes it clear that emotional healing is active, not passive. This supports the main thesis by showing how fantasy elements in anime, like magical doors and monsters, can be used to explore very real emotional truths. The film uses this symbolism to express how people can confront the past, accept it, and grow from it (Meng).
