Ambiguity and Pacing

Subthesis 2: Symbols of Loss and Love

The second subthesis evidence from Suzume comes from the scene at 00:30:00. One of the main characters, Souta, unexpectedly transforms into a small, three-legged chair. At first, this change is confusing—the film does not clearly explain the magical rules of the world or why this transformation happens. However, the emotional weight of the moment is made clear when Souta says:

“I don’t know why I became this chair either. But it feels like I’m stuck here for a reason… maybe because this chair means something to you?”

This is one of the most symbolic scenes in the film. Even though the magical reasoning is unclear, the emotional meaning behind the chair is powerful. The chair was handmade and gifted to Suzume by her late mother. It is small, worn, and missing a leg, but it is also full of love and memory. It is the last object Suzume has that connects her to her mother. When Souta becomes the chair, the film suggests that the emotions Suzume has attached to this object—her grief, her longing, and her love—are still alive. The transformation makes the chair a living symbol of how the past can shape the present and how memory can take on new life through others (Fitaloka et al.; Meng et al.).

Later in the film, Suzume risks everything to save Souta, just as she once clung to the chair as a way to stay close to her mother. This shows how powerful personal items can become symbols of emotional survival. Through the chair, we see how people carry their grief through physical things, but also how those things can become a path to healing. The chair is no longer just furniture—it becomes a symbol of both loss and love. This moment supports the thesis by showing that grief is not only found in memories, but also in objects that hold deep meaning, and that these symbols can help people move forward (DoctorKev).
